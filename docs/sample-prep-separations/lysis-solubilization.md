# 1.1 Lysis and Solubilization for Intact Proteins

The very first question you face is how to get your proteins out of cells (or tissues, or biofluids) and into a state where a mass spectrometer can actually measure them. This sounds simple. It is not simple. Intact proteins are hydrophobic, sticky, prone to aggregation, and extremely fond of any surface they can find — and the reagents that solve those problems best (SDS, Triton, high-salt buffers) are precisely the reagents that wreck electrospray.

The workhorse approach for top-down has traditionally been chaotrope-based lysis: 6–8 M urea or guanidinium chloride, sometimes with organic solvents like methanol or trifluoroethanol. These denature proteins thoroughly and, crucially, can be removed or diluted before MS.

One increasingly popular family of cleanup methods is bead-based aggregation capture. **SP3** (single-pot solid-phase-enhanced sample preparation), developed by Hughes and colleagues for bottom-up proteomics,[^hughes2019] uses hydrophilic carboxylate-coated beads to trap proteins in ethanol-driven aggregates while contaminants wash away; **USP** adapted the idea to recover *intact* proteins by eluting from the beads with cold formic acid,[^dagley2019] and **SP4** drops the beads entirely, using acetonitrile-induced aggregation and centrifugation for better recovery of hydrophobic, low-solubility proteins.[^johnston2022] These are genuinely useful tools — but they are options, not a default. Which method wins depends heavily on your sample and your downstream goal, so it's worth grounding the choice in the 2024 benchmarking study by Kaulich and colleagues[^kaulich2024] and the field's best-practices and benchmarks paper[^donnelly2019] rather than reaching for whatever is trendy. For clinical applications, the 2025 review by Kamal and colleagues is the most current synthesis.[^kamal2025]

If you take one practical point away: **the right lysis buffer depends on your downstream separation and your mass range of interest**. Samples destined for direct infusion or native MS need very different handling than samples going into denaturing LC-MS, and what works beautifully for a 15 kDa cytosolic protein may fail badly for a 60 kDa membrane protein. Match the prep to the target.

## Getting the detergent back out

The flip side of using a good solubilizer is having to remove it. **SDS is the most effective denaturing detergent and, for exactly that reason, the most troublesome** — it ionizes beautifully in ESI, suppresses your analyte, and fouls the source. In practice, detergent–MS incompatibility is the failure mode most newcomers hit first; SDS interferes with both bottom-up and top-down analysis and must be depleted before the protein ever reaches the instrument.[^unterlander2018]

The hard part is removing the detergent *without* losing the intact protein along with it. A head-to-head comparison of the common routes — filter-aided sample prep (FASP), acetone precipitation, and transmembrane electrophoresis (TME) — found that all three strip out >99.8% of SDS, but their intact-protein recoveries diverge sharply: roughly 90% for TME, ~55% for a FASP protocol modified to elute intact protein, and ~57% for acetone precipitation.[^unterlander2018] Two things are worth internalizing there. First, the popular spin-filter and trap methods (FASP, S-Trap) are fundamentally *digest-on-membrane* (bottom-up) platforms — the protein is meant to be proteolyzed in place, not eluted whole — so getting intact protein back out takes a modified protocol and costs recovery. Second, even "gentle" acetone precipitation quietly loses ~40% of your sample. For denaturing top-down specifically, membrane ultrafiltration (buffer exchange across a molecular-weight-cutoff filter) is a practical SDS-removal route, and for membrane proteins, adding methanol during electrophoretic depletion markedly improves recovery.[^said2024]

Two cleanup workhorses deserve singling out. **Methanol/chloroform/water (MCW) precipitation** — the classic Wessel–Flügge method — adds methanol, then chloroform, then water to your sample to set up a biphasic system; protein collects at the interphase while detergents, lipids, salts, buffers, and reducing agents partition away into the surrounding solvents.[^wessel1984] It is fast (minutes, not an overnight acetone step), works on micro- to milliliter volumes, and recovers hydrophobic proteins that simple acetone precipitation tends to lose — which is exactly why it remains a perennial favorite for stripping detergent ahead of electrophoresis and MS. The catch is the one that haunts all precipitation: redissolving the interphase can be hard for large or very hydrophobic proteoforms (see [the resolubilization problem](#the-resolubilization-problem-after-precipitation) below), so top-down workflows usually take the pellet back up in a strong acid/organic system such as cold formic acid.

**Solid-phase extraction (SPE)** is the other mainstay: trap the protein on a reversed-phase sorbent (C4, C2, or polymeric PLRP), wash detergent and salt to waste, and elute in organic. It is the same principle as the trap-and-elute desalting built into many LC front-ends, and SPE sorbents demonstrably strip even high concentrations of SDS and Triton X-100.[^chen2014] Integrated devices push this further by combining precipitation and SPE in one cartridge — the Doucette lab's two-stage spin cartridge (later commercialized as the ProTrap XG) retained >95% of protein while depleting >99.7% of SDS.[^crowell2014] Worth an honest flag: most of the *rigorously published* SPE and integrated-device characterization is at the peptide (bottom-up) level — a dedicated intact-protein RP-SPE landmark is oddly missing — but the approach is thoroughly standard practice for getting a detergent-laden protein MS-ready. Quicker spot fixes — KCl precipitation of SDS (the potassium salt of dodecyl sulfate is insoluble and simply crashes out) and commercial detergent-removal spin columns — are handy at small scale, though firm intact-protein recovery numbers for them are scarce.

## Cleavable surfactants help less than you'd hope

A tempting shortcut is the **acid-labile, MS-cleavable surfactant** class — RapiGest SF, ProteaseMAX, PPS Silent Surfactant — which solubilize during prep and then break down on acidification into MS-friendlier fragments. The important caveat: these were designed and optimized to improve enzymatic *digestion*, and essentially all of their validation is peptide-level — digestion completeness, missed cleavages, peptide identifications.[^norrgran2009] Their benefit for *intact* proteins is real but more limited than the bottom-up literature might lead you to expect. Tellingly, when the top-down community wanted a surfactant of its own, it didn't reach for RapiGest — it developed a *photo*cleavable surfactant, **Azo**, specifically for top-down: Azo solubilizes proteins comparably to SDS, degrades under UV light into MS-compatible products, and enabled top-down characterization of membrane proteins and their modifications.[^brown2019] If you need detergent-grade solubilization in a top-down workflow, Azo is the more purpose-built choice.

## The resolubilization problem after precipitation

Organic-solvent precipitation (acetone, TCA, chloroform/methanol) is the default way to concentrate dilute samples and wash away salts and detergents — but **what comes down does not always go back up**. TCA-precipitated protein is trapped in a stable molten-globule state that resists redissolving in aqueous buffer; conventional acetone or ethanol washes then lose a large fraction of it, and only a dedicated ethanol/HCl wash that unfolds the molten globule recovered material from ~50% back up to ~96%.[^eddhif2018] Recovery is also strongly method-dependent — systematic comparisons of acetone, chloroform/methanol/water, TCA-acetone, and related schemes show several-fold differences in yield between protocols.[^saraygord2015] In intact-protein terms, the ~57% acetone-precipitation recovery noted above is the same story.[^unterlander2018] The practical upshot — and this is hard-won lab lore more than a single clean result — is that **large and hydrophobic proteins resolubilize worst**: the bigger and stickier the proteoform, the more of it you should expect to leave behind in the pellet. Plan for it, and skip an unnecessary precipitation step when you can.
