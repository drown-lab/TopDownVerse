# 1.6 Carryover, Recovery, and Adsorptive Loss

This is the unglamorous topic that eats more method-development time than almost anything else, and the one a community knowledgebase can genuinely help with — because much of it never makes it into a paper. Intact proteins are sticky. They adsorb to metal, glass, and plastic; they smear from one injection into the next; and they quietly vanish somewhere between the vial and the source at exactly the moment you're trying to quantify them. None of it is mysterious, but all of it is fiddly, and most of what you'll learn here you'll learn the hard way unless someone tells you first.

A note on the evidence below: the cleanest quantitative data on adsorption and surface chemistry come from monoclonal antibodies, acidic peptides, and oligonucleotides, and a fair amount of it is published by instrument and column manufacturers (legitimate peer-reviewed work, but promoting their own hardware). That small- and mid-size intact proteins behave the same way is reasonable and widely observed, but it is often *extrapolation* rather than a directly measured result — so treat the specifics as well-founded guidance, not gospel.

## Adsorptive loss: where the protein goes

Protein is lost all along the flow path, not just on the column. Irreversible loss onto stainless-steel column **frits** was documented as far back as the 1980s, and it gets proportionally worse as the injected amount drops toward ~1 µg and below — exactly the regime of low-abundance proteoform work.[^sadek1985] Two mechanisms are worth separating. The first is ordinary hydrophobic and electrostatic adsorption to surfaces, which hits large, hydrophobic, "sticky" proteins hardest (the wide-pore, short-chain choices in [§1.3 — Reversed-Phase LC](./reversed-phase-lc.md) are partly about exactly this). The second is **metal-mediated** adsorption: leached metal ions and metal-oxide surfaces grab analytes bearing phosphate or multiple carboxylates, which is why acidic peptides, nucleotides, and phosphoproteins are notoriously hard to recover.[^hsiao2018] A useful tell is that losses are usually worst on the *first* injection through a fresh flow path and partially "condition out" with repeat runs — for one phosphorothioate oligonucleotide, an inert surface improved recovery by ~73% over standard hardware, most dramatically on initial use.[^nguyenoligo2021]

## Taming the metal: passivation and inert hardware

The hardware response is to make the flow path less reactive. **Hybrid-surface and bio-inert hardware** — frits, tubing, and columns coated with an ethylene-bridged hybrid silica or similar — measurably improve recovery: in a direct frit comparison, a hydrophilic hybrid surface beat unmodified hybrid, titanium, stainless steel, and PEEK for protein recovery and repeatability.[^fekete2022] A bio-inert SEC column of the same chemistry, coupled to MS, recovered high-mass aggregate species and reproduced better than a stainless-steel column packed with identical media — directly relevant to native and intact-protein LC-MS.[^murisier2022] For metal-sensitive analytes you can also passivate *chemically*: adding **medronic acid** (a diphosphonic acid) at micromolar levels to the mobile phase competes for metal binding sites and restores peak shape and signal.[^hsiao2018] On RPLC specifically, hybrid-silica surfaces cut metal-induced tailing of acidic peptides by up to ~80% while leaving non-metal-sensitive peptides unchanged — a reminder that these fixes are analyte-specific.[^birdsall2021] If you want to know whether your system even *needs* any of this, a metal-sensitive probe compound injected with no column (such as AMPcP) is a simple readiness test for exposed metal in the flow path.[^simeone2022] One honest caveat: "metal-free" is not automatically better — titanium and PEEK frits were *not* clearly superior to stainless steel for protein recovery in the head-to-head above.[^fekete2022]

## Carryover and how to chase it

Carryover — signal from one sample bleeding into the next — shares a root cause with adsorption: analyte sticking somewhere it shouldn't and releasing later. The usual suspects are the autosampler needle and seat, the injection valve, the sample loop and any tubing with dead volume, and the column. Even an inline **frit can be the culprit**, as in a screening method where a stainless-steel filter caused compound-specific carryover that vanished when the frit was replaced.[^kim2023] Mitigation is mostly disciplined plumbing and washing: minimize dead volume and tubing length, use an appropriately strong needle/seat wash (often a strong-solvent followed by weak-solvent two-stage wash), run blank injections between samples, and monitor carryover against your lower limit of quantitation. Those wash-and-blank practices are codified in bioanalytical guidance and vendor protocols rather than any single primary paper, so treat them as established best practice. And remember that carryover is frequently an analyte-solubility problem in disguise — keeping a sticky protein genuinely in solution often does more than another wash cycle.

## A practical recovery checklist

- **Use deactivated, low-bind sample vials and tips.** Silanized (deactivated) glass vials sharply reduced losses of the notoriously sticky peptide hepcidin-25; the same logic applies to any low-abundance proteoform.[^abbas2018]
- **Treat solvent and pH as recovery levers, not just chromatography knobs.** Adjusting additive chemistry and pH to keep the analyte in solution and off surfaces is a first-line fix; medronic acid is the metal-specific version of the same idea.[^abbas2018][^hsiao2018]
- **Minimize exposed surface area and dead volume,** and prefer deactivated/hybrid frits, columns, and hardware at low load.[^sadek1985][^fekete2022][^birdsall2021]
- **Condition the system** with priming injections — and, practitioner lore but widely used, consider a sacrificial carrier protein to occupy adsorption sites, low-bind pipette tips, and avoiding overly aqueous diluents for hydrophobic proteins.

These last items are hard-won habits more than published results, which is exactly why they belong in a community resource: nobody publishes "we lost half our sample to the autosampler until we changed vials," but everybody learns it eventually.
